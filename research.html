K-Cohomology

From the algebro-geometric perspective, algebraic K-theory is given by a sequence of abelian groups which yields arithmetic and geometric information about a scheme through its category of vector bundles or coherent sheaves. The association of the group $K_i(X)$ to a scheme $X$ may be viewed as a sheaf (after Zariski sheafification). The study of K-cohomology is then just the study of the corresponding cohomology groups $H^p(X, K_i)$. This doubly indexed sequence of cohomology groups computes the K-theory of $X$ via the Brown-Gersten-Quillen spectral sequence.

Markus Rost has developed a formalism for defining K-cohomology groups using the language of cycle modules (which also happen to be examples of unramified sheaves with transfers). This theory was inspired by the topological situation, where varying the coefficients in cohomology yields interesting information about a given topological space. This formalism makes use of Milnor K-theory, a more explicit and computable sequence of groups which encodes some of the same information as Quillen's algebraic K-groups.

My current research focuses on computing the group of zero cycles with coefficients in the sheaf $K_1$ (which coincides with the group $H^d(X, K_{d+1})$, where $d = \dim(X)$) for generalized Severi-Brauer varieties. These varieties are defined as the collection of ideals in a central simple algebra of a specified dimension. They are twisted forms of Grassmannians, i.e., over an algebraically closed field, generalized Severi-Brauer varieties and Grassmannians are one and the same. Over arbitrary fields, these varieties may fail to have any (geometric) points.
Patching and K-Theory

Harbater, Hartmann and Krashen have developed a new method of patching which has been used to prove results in Galois theory over functions fields of curves. By working over fraction fields rather than over rings, one focuses on vector spaces as opposed to modules, providing a conceptually simpler tool for obtaining gluing results. These new methods have led to local-global principles for Brauer and Galois cohomology groups, quadratic forms, and subfields of division algebras.

My current research in this area focuses on whether similar local-global principles exist for various flavors of algebraic K-theory, e.g., Quillen, Milnor, or étale.
